## Interview #1 — Maria Aksarina

**Date:** March 2026
**Profile:** 46 years old. Architect at a British bureau in London. Married, one child (now school-age). Lives in Bromley in a 1930s semi-detached house purchased in 2020 for ~£500K. Russian-speaking, has been in London for ~13 years.

### Their situation in 2026

Maria and her husband have now lived in the Bromley house for six years. The initial plan was always to renovate — move the toilet to the first floor, open up the kitchen, possibly extend — but life and budget kept pushing it back. Their child started school nearby (a factor that now anchors them to the area), and the mortgage is locked for another year. The house has appreciated to roughly £580–600K on paper, but they've done almost nothing structurally. The garden is well-kept, the cosmetics are fine, but the layout still frustrates her daily. The extension still doesn't have planning permission because they never got around to applying.

She's been following the Renters' Rights Act 2026 discussions because a colleague at the bureau is doing social housing refurbishment projects and keeps talking about how post-Grenfell regulations have tightened everything. Maria notices that construction costs have climbed — a kitchen extension that might have cost £35K in 2020 now quotes at £50–65K. She's nervous about committing.

### What she said

"We bought this house because during COVID we realised we needed space — not a flat, a house. Six years later, the space is the same and we still haven't done the things we said we would. The toilet is still in the wrong place. The extension is still a lean-to. The architect in me is embarrassed, honestly."

She searched Rightmove recently — not to move, but to see what renovated houses in Bromley sell for versus unrenovated ones. The gap shocked her. Houses with proper extensions and modern kitchens in her postcode sell for £700–750K. Hers, in its current state, would go for £580K at best. There's a £120–150K gap and she estimates the work would cost £80–100K. The maths works. But she doesn't know how to start without it consuming her life.

"I need someone to tell me: here's the realistic scope, here's what it costs in 2026, here's the phasing so we can live in the house during works. And please don't lie to me about the timeline."

### Pain points

- She understands buildings professionally but finds managing her own renovation paralysing — the emotional stakes are different when it's your own home
- Construction cost inflation since 2020 has made her cautious — every quote feels unreliable
- She wants a phased plan, not an all-or-nothing commitment
- Rightmove doesn't tell her the value gap between renovated and unrenovated — she had to research comparables manually
- Agents are useless for this kind of strategic question — they only care when she's ready to sell

### Relevance to Piega

Maria is the "aesthete who already knows buildings" — she doesn't need Piega to tell her what a Victorian terrace is. She needs it to give her the financial confidence to act. The phased renovation assessment, the realistic budget bar, and the area comparables are exactly what would have moved her from "we should do this" to "let's book the architect."

Simon validates the product hypothesis from a builder's perspective. His observation about historical listings confirms a gap we can fill. His pricing intuition (£5/analysis or £20 bundle) provides concrete commercial direction. His budget is modest for London (£400–500K), which means flats, not houses. He's been looking at areas like Walthamstow, Tottenham, and Stratford — regeneration zones where prices are still accessible but growth is expected.

What makes Ashish uniquely relevant is that he understands AI products deeply. He can evaluate Piega not just as a user but as a builder. He's shipped ML systems at Revolut that onboarded 40K+ customers daily. He knows what works in AI-powered products and what doesn't.

### What he said

"I'm interested in your product from two angles — as a potential user and as someone who builds AI products for a living. Let me give you both."

"As a user: I've been browsing Rightmove for three months. I see a flat in Walthamstow for £420K and I think — is that good? Is the area going up? What's the rental yield if I rent it out later? Is it near the Elizabeth Line? I end up with 15 browser tabs open trying to answer questions that should be in one place. Your tool sounds like it consolidates that."

"As a builder: be careful about what you promise. The biggest mistake AI products make is overfitting to impressive demos. Your 'Beautiful Wreck' analysis might look incredible in a screenshot, but if someone pastes a real Rightmove link and the output is generic or wrong, you've lost them forever. The hardest part isn't making it beautiful — it's making it accurate and specific for every property type, every area, every construction era. That's a data problem, not a model problem."

"From my Revolut experience — the products that won were the ones that worked for the 80th percentile user, not the 99th percentile. Don't build for Denis the HMO expert. Build for me — someone who's smart, has money, understands tech, but knows nothing about UK property."

His practical suggestion: use the Piega analysis as a lead-gen tool. Give the surface assessment free. Charge for depth. But make the free version so good that people screenshot it and share it — that's your growth loop. "At Nativ, our best marketing is when someone shares a culturalized piece of content and people say 'how did they do that?' Your version is when someone shares a property analysis and people say 'wait, that looks professional — where did you get it?'"

### Relevance to Piega

Ashish is the dream early adopter and potential advisor. He's our ideal user (tech professional, London buyer, first-time house hunter), and he's also an AI builder who can evaluate our product architecture. His XYZ Reality connection to Ilia creates a warm introduction path. His a16z network could open doors if the product shows traction. And his advice — "build for the 80th percentile, make the free version shareable" — is immediately actionable product strategy.

## Expressed Interest in Testing / Subscribing

Five interviewees said they'd be willing to test Piega and consider a paid subscription — but each with different conditions and expectations.

### 1. Dmitry Ro — "Give me precision, I'll pay per house"

**Willing to pay:** Yes, immediately — but per-analysis, not monthly.

**His terms:** He'd start by running 4–6 analyses on specific houses he's already shortlisted in the N6/N10/N2 area. If the first analysis is accurate enough that it matches what he later finds during a viewing, he's in. If it's vague or generic, he's out permanently.

**What he expects to get:**
- Street-level comparable data: unrenovated vs. renovated sale prices on his target streets
- Renovation cost breakdown by trade (structural, M&E, finishes) — not a single number, a real breakdown
- Materials quality assessment: what's the existing build quality, what needs replacing vs. what's cosmetic
- A "confidence score" for each estimate — he doesn't mind wide ranges, he minds dishonesty

**Price sensitivity:** Not at all. Would pay £20–30 per analysis without hesitation. Said: "If someone charges me £5, I won't trust it. If they charge me £50 and the output is specific to my actual house on my actual street, I'll pay it five times over."

**Risk for us:** He's a perfectionist. If the output falls short of his standards, he'll be vocal about it. But if it meets them, he'll become a power user and a referrer.

## Key Themes Across All 12 Interviews

**1. Renovation cost uncertainty is universal.** From Maria (architect) to Ben (serial entrepreneur with 15 properties) — nobody trusts renovation cost estimates. This is Piega's core value proposition.

**2. The "value gap" is real but invisible.** Multiple interviewees described the gap between unrenovated and renovated comparables in their area — but none of them could find this data easily. Piega should surface it.

**3. Historical listing data is a hidden goldmine.** Simon built a Telegram bot for it. Alex misses it from his agency days. Dmitry would use it to validate hypotheses. Rightmove hides it.

**4. Area intelligence beats property intelligence.** Denis, Arman, Alex, and Ashish all want to understand areas — not just buildings. Flood risk, demographics, transport, school quality, HMO licensing rules, regeneration plans. This is what separates Piega from a renovation calculator.

**5. Two distinct segments emerge.** "Aesthetes" (Maria, Aleksei, Polina, Dmitry) want to buy/renovate beautiful buildings and need financial confidence. "Investors" (Denis, Arman, Alex, Ben) want yield and need regulatory/area intelligence. Piega can serve both but the entry point is different.

**6. The product must be honest about its limits.** Tom and Ashish both warned: don't hallucinate. Show confidence levels. Tell the user what you don't know. This is what builds trust — and what separates Piega from generic AI.

**7. Pricing signals are clear.** Simon: £5/analysis or £20 bundle. Ashish: free surface, paid depth. Ben: £500/year for portfolio operators. The three-tier model emerges naturally from the user segments.
